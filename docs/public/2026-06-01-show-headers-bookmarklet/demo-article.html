Long articles often ask the reader to keep several ideas in mind at once: the current section, the parent topic, and the next place where the argument changes direction.

Why headings matter

Headings are the map of a document. They turn a continuous page into a set of named places, and they give readers a way to return after scanning, searching, or stepping away.

Local context

A reader usually needs the nearest headings, not the full table of contents. The useful question is often simple: what section am I in right now?

When pages get hard to follow

Documentation, essays, and reference pages can be clear in small pieces but difficult to navigate after several screens of scrolling.

Interruptions

After an interruption, a compact heading navigator can help a reader regain orientation without returning to the top of the page.

How a floating navigator helps

The navigator follows the document structure while staying available in the current tab. It is small enough to stay out of the way and direct enough to answer the reader's next navigation question.

Nearby headings

Showing a few headings above and below the current section keeps the display useful without becoming another full page outline.

Practical limits

The bookmarklet depends on real heading elements. It is most useful on pages that use h1 through h6 to describe the document structure.
