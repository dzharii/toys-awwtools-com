A00

* Ensure that the page has one clearly dominant working area, and ensure that this working area is the catalog-detail relationship rather than the surrounding utility panels.

  * Ensure that the catalog and detail views visually read as the primary decision surface.
  * Ensure that recommendation, quick views, topic-guide shortcuts, and progress summary do not visually overpower the catalog-detail workspace.
  * Ensure that a user can identify the main working area within a few seconds without interpreting the whole page.

* Ensure that the overall information architecture feels like one coherent product rather than a stack of unrelated modules.

  * Ensure that recommendation, progression, catalog, and detail surfaces visibly belong to the same conceptual system.
  * Ensure that the page reads as one guided workspace and not as a dashboard with many equal-weight panels.
  * Ensure that neighboring sections explain their relationship through layout, alignment, or connective design rather than only by being placed near each other.

* Ensure that the progression map is visible by default and no longer hidden behind a collapsed disclosure state on initial load.

  * Ensure that the progression map is expanded by default.
  * Ensure that the progression map is visible in the first meaningful viewport on desktop, or appears immediately after a very small amount of scrolling.
  * Ensure that the progression map is treated as a first-class navigation and orientation tool rather than a secondary extra.
  * Ensure that the progression map does not feel like decorative compliance.

* Ensure that the progression map is directly connected to the rest of the product behavior.

  * Ensure that selecting a stage, cluster, or node in the progression map changes the catalog view, visible slice, or selected context in a meaningful and predictable way.
  * Ensure that selecting a problem in the catalog can be reflected on the progression map.
  * Ensure that the current visible slice, selected problem, or active phase can be understood partly through the map.
  * Ensure that the progression map helps the user answer where a problem belongs in the learning journey.

* Ensure that the left-side orientation area is visually quieter and no longer competes with the main workspace.

  * Ensure that quick-view entries are presented as lighter route items, grouped controls, or other low-noise navigation elements rather than visually heavy mini cards.
  * Ensure that the left rail helps with orientation and route entry without attracting more attention than the catalog or detail panel.
  * Ensure that the left rail content remains useful while reducing visual mass and box repetition.

* Ensure that the recommendation layer is compact, differentiated, and editorially decisive.

  * Ensure that the recommendation area clearly highlights one primary recommendation when appropriate.
  * Ensure that secondary recommendations are visibly subordinate and not just repeated copies of the same structure.
  * Ensure that recommendation cards or panels do not repeat nearly identical copy without adding decision value.
  * Ensure that the recommendation layer reduces choice friction instead of merely presenting more choices.

* Ensure that the catalog uses a stable, scrollable workspace pattern on larger screens.

  * Ensure that the catalog has its own scroll context on desktop or large-layout views.
  * Ensure that the user can browse the catalog without losing the currently selected detail context.
  * Ensure that the catalog does not require the whole page to behave like one long scrolling document during core decision-making.
  * Ensure that scroll behavior feels deliberate, stable, and easy to follow.

* Ensure that the selected problem state is unmistakable in the catalog.

  * Ensure that the selected row, card, or item has a strong active state through contrast, border treatment, background treatment, positional cue, or a combination of these.
  * Ensure that the selected state remains obvious even after surrounding items are scanned quickly.
  * Ensure that a user never has to ask which problem is currently selected.
  * Ensure that the visual relationship between the selected item and the detail pane is clear.

* Ensure that the detail panel is designed as a reading surface rather than a stack of generic admin boxes.

  * Ensure that the detail panel has stronger typographic rhythm, better section spacing, and less unnecessary internal boxing.
  * Ensure that the detail panel feels trustworthy, calm, and easy to read for more than a few seconds.
  * Ensure that the detail content is structured around meaningful reading questions such as why now, what this trains, what to expect, local support, nearby alternatives, and topic guides.
  * Ensure that the detail panel visually echoes the problem's place in the progression or concept system where useful.

* Ensure that the detail panel is conceptually connected to the global structure of the site.

  * Ensure that the detail view can indicate where the selected problem sits in the progression or topic landscape.
  * Ensure that the detail view can reference neighboring options, nearby alternatives, or stage context.
  * Ensure that the selected problem does not feel isolated from the rest of the product.

* Ensure that the catalog is not just a conventional list, but a curated problem workspace.

  * Ensure that the catalog rows or cards foreground title, high-value editorial summary, and the most decision-relevant metadata.
  * Ensure that low-value metadata does not dominate the catalog visually.
  * Ensure that the catalog groups or stage headers are meaningful and help the user understand why problems are arranged as they are.
  * Ensure that the catalog feels more like a structured route through the problem space than an arbitrary feed.

* Ensure that the relationship between the progression map and the catalog is visible in the layout.

  * Ensure that progression stages and catalog sections align conceptually and, where possible, spatially.
  * Ensure that the catalog can be interpreted as a visible slice of the larger progression landscape.
  * Ensure that a user can move from map to list to detail without feeling that they have switched into a different product.

* Ensure that the page uses a more distinctive conceptual identity rather than a generic dashboard identity.

  * Ensure that the design clearly supports either a field-guide mental model, an operator-workspace mental model, or an intentional blend of the two.
  * Ensure that this identity is visible in the layout, naming, relationships between sections, and interaction patterns.
  * Ensure that the product can be described in one coherent sentence, such as a field guide for Medium problems or a workspace for choosing the next problem.
  * Ensure that the identity is supported by structure rather than by decorative branding copy alone.

* Ensure that the overall visual style is calmer, sharper, and more engineered rather than soft, mushy, or overly polite.

  * Ensure that text contrast is strong enough for comfortable reading.
  * Ensure that surfaces, separators, and edges are disciplined and not overly softened everywhere.
  * Ensure that the page does not rely on excessive softness, rounded rectangles, or washed-out treatment to appear modern.
  * Ensure that the interface feels precise and intentional for a software-engineering audience.

* Ensure that the palette is controlled and supports focus rather than distraction.

  * Ensure that the base neutrals are clean and consistent.
  * Ensure that accent colors are used sparingly and purposefully for selected state, progress, active filtering, and other meaningful states.
  * Ensure that the palette does not drift into decorative pastel clutter or low-contrast fog.
  * Ensure that important information can step forward visually without requiring extra boxes or extra ornament.

* Ensure that the page reduces block repetition and generic container overuse.

  * Ensure that not every piece of information is enclosed in another rounded rectangle.
  * Ensure that spacing, alignment, hierarchy, and separation lines are used where they can replace redundant cards.
  * Ensure that block treatment is reserved for content that genuinely needs enclosure.
  * Ensure that the page no longer feels like a sea of same-weight boxes.

* Ensure that metadata styling is restrained and mature.

  * Ensure that pills, chips, and badges are used only when they materially improve scanning.
  * Ensure that low-value metadata is not turned into decorative visual noise.
  * Ensure that chips have proper text fit, spacing, and contrast when they are retained.
  * Ensure that some metadata is presented as plain text, compact labels, or structured rows where that is cleaner than pills.

* Ensure that typography carries more of the hierarchy than boxes do.

  * Ensure that headings, subheadings, support text, metadata, and explanatory text have a clear typographic system.
  * Ensure that large display text is used sparingly and only when it earns its space.
  * Ensure that text density supports reading rather than visual fatigue.
  * Ensure that line height, paragraph rhythm, and spacing between logical chunks are tuned for a content-heavy interface.

* Ensure that the opening area is concise, useful, and not theatrically oversized.

  * Ensure that the introductory statement does not consume excessive space relative to the utility it provides.
  * Ensure that the top area explains the product quickly and then gets out of the way.
  * Ensure that search and the highest-value controls are immediately useful.
  * Ensure that the opening area contributes to focus instead of delaying the real task.

* Ensure that all surrounding utility surfaces support the main task rather than competing with it.

  * Ensure that the visible-slice summary, topic-guide shortcuts, and similar utility elements are placed and weighted in a way that supports orientation.
  * Ensure that the user does not have to visually negotiate multiple equally important side panels before starting work.
  * Ensure that utility elements are subordinate to the main decision flow.

* Ensure that topic-guide entry points are integrated into the same system rather than appearing as unrelated links.

  * Ensure that topic-guide shortcuts feel attached to the progression, quick views, or selected problem context.
  * Ensure that topic guides are discoverable without becoming noisy.
  * Ensure that topic-guide links reinforce the learning map instead of sitting as isolated utility items.

* Ensure that the page uses connective design to explain relationships between major areas.

  * Ensure that there are subtle but meaningful visual links between recommendation, progression, catalog, and detail surfaces.
  * Ensure that these links can be expressed through alignment, stage markers, path cues, shared headers, side rails, background lanes, breadcrumbs, or other non-block devices.
  * Ensure that the user can visually understand how one area of the page leads into another.
  * Ensure that connective elements improve comprehension without becoming decorative clutter.

* Ensure that the design is innovative in a useful way rather than novel for its own sake.

  * Ensure that uniqueness comes from conceptual clarity, spatial logic, and product identity rather than ornamental graphics.
  * Ensure that any non-standard layout choice helps the user orient, compare, or commit more easily.
  * Ensure that innovation does not add visual confusion or cognitive overhead.

* Ensure that the page helps software engineers focus on content and not on interface friction.

  * Ensure that the design reduces scanning fatigue.
  * Ensure that the layout supports quick comparison and confident selection.
  * Ensure that the user does not have to continuously rediscover where controls, context, or selected content live.
  * Ensure that the product feels like a serious technical tool rather than a marketing surface.

* Ensure that the current design can be defended through concrete user tasks.

  * Ensure that a user can quickly locate the progression map without hunting for hidden disclosure content.
  * Ensure that a user can identify the current recommendation under the active view with little effort.
  * Ensure that a user can select a problem and immediately understand what changed.
  * Ensure that a user can browse a catalog slice while preserving the selected detail context.
  * Ensure that a user can understand how a chosen problem fits into a broader progression or topic sequence.

* Ensure that the final composition has a clear visual hierarchy from top to bottom.

  * Ensure that orientation comes before exploration, and exploration before deep inspection, unless a different sequence is intentionally justified.
  * Ensure that large visual elements earn their prominence.
  * Ensure that repetitive structures do not flatten the hierarchy.
  * Ensure that the page reads with a controlled rhythm rather than as a set of equally loud blocks.

* Ensure that the final design can pass a practical quality review.

  * Ensure that the page looks more coherent after ten seconds of viewing, not more confusing.
  * Ensure that the page feels more focused than distracting.
  * Ensure that the page feels more unique than generic.
  * Ensure that the page feels more convenient than merely feature-complete.
  * Ensure that the layout communicates a strong point of view about how this product should be used.
